Orbit, A Fancy Planner

A circular 24-hour planner for visual time blocking, recurring routines, templates, and drag-based daily scheduling. Built around the shape of a day instead of a linear list.

JavaScript Planner Web App iOS
App Store Launch Web App Read More Privacy Policy
Why I Made This

I built Orbit because traditional planners never worked particularly well for my ADHD or the way I actually schedule my life.

A lot of tasks do not happen on a strict weekly cadence. Instead, they need to happen five days after I last did them or two weeks from completion. Orbit's regenerating task system was built around that idea and remains my favorite feature.

I also wanted a better way to see where my time is actually going throughout the day instead of constantly jumping between calendars and task lists.

Time Since

Track how long it's been since chores, maintenance, routines, and life events. Instead of recurring on a fixed schedule, reminders regenerate based on the last time something was actually completed.

Productivity ADHD Habits Web App
Launch App GitHub Repo
Why I Made This

I built this because many recurring tasks do not fit a normal weekly calendar.

Replacing HVAC filters, changing sheets, cleaning litterboxes, watering plants, fasting sessions, and dozens of other routines are often better tracked from the last completion date rather than from a fixed schedule.

Time Since supports occurrence trackers, session trackers, counters, reminders, CSV import/export, and flexible elapsed-time views ranging from days to years.

Better NYC Map

A web-based NYC transit map and route planner focused on real commuting tradeoffs: walking thresholds, transfer tolerance, above-ground preferences, buses, ferries, PATH, LIRR, Metro-North, and best-case versus worst-case route estimates.

NYC Transit Maps Routing Web App
GitHub Repo
Why I Made This

I wanted a transit planner that reflects how people actually make NYC routing decisions, not just the mathematically shortest trip.

The project is designed around options like walking farther to avoid a transfer, preferring above-ground routes, mixing subways with buses or ferries, and comparing best-case versus worst-case transfer-heavy trips.

Longer-term, it can support native app packaging, service-alert-aware routing, accessibility preferences, and better multi-modal planning across NYC and regional transit.
